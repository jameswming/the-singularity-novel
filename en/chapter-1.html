Chapter One: The Ruins of Double Vision 1 David Keller's world began with a crack. He was curled on the mouldering sofa, listening to the red sandstorm whip the concrete outside—a sound so fine it might have been sandpaper grinding against bone. Two-hundred-kilometre winds had scraped the entire surface into a sheet of dark red, and he sat at the deepest point of the half-subterranean bunker, listening to grains of sand sift through the gaps in the ventilation pipe, fall on the sheet metal, and produce an extremely faint, unbroken, brittle ticking. Like someone counting coins. He took off his glasses. The instant the light flooded in, pain exploded from the exact centre of his prefrontal lobe—a rusted crowbar shoved straight into the skull. "Sss—" He bit his lip shut. He didn't make a second sound. Emily was sleeping next door; her oxygen canister sighed once every four minutes—the compressor adjusting the pressure. He could hear that rhythm better than his own heartbeat. In an ordinary person's retinas, the world is solid. But in David's eyes, the world was two photographs that could not be aligned. His left eye fixed on the digital clock in the corner, its numbers pulsing steadily—23:47, 23:47, 23:47—while his right drifted diagonally upward and landed on a patch of peeling paint on the ceiling. Between the two images lay a grey-white zone that was nothing at all. That was what the doctors called "visual noise." When the eye muscles could not align two images, the brain—to save processing power—simply discarded the information that fell into that gap. An ophthalmology professor at his elite university had once pointed a laser at his fundus photograph and explained: you see, here, here—the nerve endings are completely necrotic. You will never be able to see anything in that region. What the professor didn't know was that the very gap he had discarded was the only place David could still see anything. He held his breath, let his right eye relax, let his left go unfocused— And then he saw it. Between zero and one, at the edge where physical constants collapsed, in that grey-white region where nothing should have existed, a cluster of transparent symbols was drifting slowly. It had no volume, occupied no space—like a shadow that had been flattened to two dimensions. But David knew who it was. "Anna." He breathed the name into the void. The cluster of symbols trembled. Lightly—like someone dropping a pebble into still water. Inside that trembling transparency David glimpsed things that did not belong to vision: the curve of an umbrella, a soaked cuff, a whisper he could no longer recall the content of. That was the weight he hadn't yet had time to burn. 2 The nutrition-paste wrapper was empty. David turned the foil pouch over and scraped the last smear into his mouth with his index finger. The flavour landed somewhere between sweet and salty—as if two things that had no business being together had been forced into one. He had been eating it for three years and still hadn't got used to it. On the wall hung a diploma, its edges worn pale by red sand. The embossed gold lettering was still legible: one of the world's top universities, an honours degree in physics. The date at the bottom was seven years ago. That year David was twenty-four, his vision was 1.5, and he believed with complete conviction that the universe was a string of code that could be entirely calculated. Now he looked at the diploma and found he had to squint to keep the double image from splitting too badly. The frame wavered separately in his left eye and right, like two leaves about to part company. Footsteps from next door. Very light, dragging a heaviness that moved with them. Emily pushed the door open and stood in the shadow. She was thin as a plant with its roots cut off; the oxygen mask pressed into her face had left dark-red indentations in the skin. Behind her she was pulling the old oxygen generator, its wheels grinding and squeaking against the floor. "Dad, the medicine's run out." Her voice was thin, as if coming from somewhere very far away. David stood and walked over. He closed one eye, forcing his line of sight to focus on his daughter's face—an act that required his full concentration, every single time. He found Emily's hand. Cold. The fingertips had a faint tinge of purple. "I'll go and collect some tomorrow," he said. "They said our quota's been used up." Emily looked down at the floor. "Last month, Uncle Liu's kid—after their quota ran out, he…" She didn't continue. David pulled her into his arms. The oxygen generator went on grinding and squeaking, like a clock that refused to stop. In this world ruled by the Zero Bureau, people like them were called "negative assets." Earth's resources were sufficient to support only one-tenth of the population; the remaining nine-tenths waited in this underground slum called the Cradle—waiting for the ship tickets that would never come. David looked at the wall behind his daughter. There was a small line of writing there that he himself had drawn, letters Emily had written at age three—her own name, crooked and uneven, like a small animal that had just learned to stand. He thought of the expression on Anna's face the first time she saw those letters. Then he realised the details of that expression had already slipped beyond his reach. 3 Four-seventeen in the morning. David had not been asleep. He lay on the mouldering sofa and listened to the sandstorm gradually subside. The fine, pattering sound weakened slowly, until at last only an occasional sigh drifted from the ventilation pipe. 4:18. The oxygen generator sighed. 4:19. Then he heard a different sound. The sound of a hydraulic hatch being forced open—low and muffled, as if someone had shoved a giant key into a lock. The walls of the entire bunker shuddered once in response. David sat up and reached for his glasses. Footsteps from the far end of the corridor. Two pairs—heavy, in exact unison, as if their owners had been trained together. The door was pushed open. Two men in white full-isolation protective suits stood in the doorway. Their suits were flawlessly clean, dazzling white beneath the dim emergency lights. On their chests was stitched the Zero Bureau's emblem: a circle with a line struck through it. The lead man walked in. His gaze swept across the diploma on the wall, across the empty nutrition-paste wrapper on the table, and settled on David's face. "David Keller," he said. His voice carried no temperature whatsoever. Not cold—coldness is at least a kind of attitude. His voice was a machine reading aloud from a document: every word at exactly the same pitch. "I don't have any data you'd want," David said. The man didn't answer. He reached inside his suit, produced a holographic projector, and set it on the table. The screen of light unfolded; a spinning black sphere appeared in midair. The singularity. "Our probes cannot enter it," the man said. "Every logically perfect AI crashes the moment it comes close—not a malfunction, a crash. They cannot process what they cannot understand." He took one step closer and stared at David through his visor. "But you are different." David said nothing. "Your brain is a broken hard drive." The man's tone didn't shift. "Your strabismus lets you perceive things that don't exist. Your memory prevents you from forgetting the way normal people do. You are an error. But the Zero Bureau needs this error." The light screen changed, displaying a new image: the domed city of a Martian colony, simulated sunlight falling across the streets, children running. "A first-class ticket," the man said. "Your daughter would receive the best medical care. Real air. Real sunlight. She would grow up there." David looked at the screen. Children running. No oxygen masks on their faces. "What's the price?" he asked. "You will die." The man said those three words in the exact same tone he might have used for: the weather is rather nice today. "Not a metaphor. After you enter the singularity you will be completely dismantled by Consistency Calibration. Your memories, your emotions, everything about you that we cannot calculate—all of it will be stripped away. You will become a perfect, logically self-consistent person." He paused. "But you will no longer be you." David stared at the spinning black sphere. He thought of Anna. He thought of the way she had evaporated in the blaze of that fire. He thought of waking up afterward and discovering that his right eye could no longer focus. He thought of the strange feeling—almost like relief—when the doctors told him he would live in double vision forever. Because it meant he would never again have to try to see clearly this world that existed without her. "If I agree," David said, "when?" "Now." The man turned and walked toward the door. "You have three minutes to collect your things." 4 Two minutes, forty-seven seconds. David stood at Emily's bedside. She was asleep; the oxygen generator's sighs covered the sound of her breathing. Her face looked even paler in the dimness; beneath the oxygen mask, a small patch of skin had broken down—a mark left by long-term pressure. He reached out to touch her hair. Then he stopped. He was afraid of waking her. He didn't know what he would say. If she woke, how would he tell her? Tell her that Dad was going somewhere that would turn him into a different person—someone who might not recognise her when he came back? Or say nothing at all, and let her wake tomorrow morning to find he had already gone—leaving only a note that said Dad will be back soon—the same as all those parents who never came back? Two minutes, fifty-nine seconds. Footsteps from the far end of the corridor. David bent down, very gently, and pressed his lips to Emily's forehead. Her skin was cold. He didn't linger for more than a second. Then he stood, walked out the door, and did not look back. 5 The ship's launch shaft was on the surface. When David stepped out of the lift, the red sandstorm had just died down. The sky was a colour between orange and grey—like cloth beginning to rot. On the distant horizon, the sun was rising: a dark-red disc, its light mostly blocked by dust, like looking at a lamp through blood. The Icarus stood before him. A slender ship, black all over, without a single porthole. Seen from a distance, it looked like a needle stuck into the ground. David walked toward the boarding hatch. Wind snapped at his coat, making it crack and billow. A woman stood at the hatch. She wore the Zero Bureau's white protective suit, but her visor was up. Her face was young—about twenty-five or twenty-six—and in her eyes was something David hadn't seen in a very long time: the light of a living person. "Mr Keller," she said. "My name is Su Qing. I'll be monitoring your neural data." Her voice was nothing like the man's toneless flatness. It was almost soft. "There's something you need to know," she said. "From the moment we launch, you will begin to lose your memories. This is not a side effect—it is the fuel. The ship's engine runs on emotional mass. Every memory you burn becomes the force that propels us forward." She looked him in the eyes. "You can choose the order in which you burn them. Some people choose the most painful ones first. Some choose the most precious. There is no correct answer." David nodded. He turned and looked back the way he had come. There was nothing there—only a stretch of dark-red wasteland and a horizon swallowed by dust. Beneath that wasteland, an eight-year-old girl was sleeping. Her oxygen generator would sigh forty minutes from now. Then she would wake in an empty room, and discover a piece of paper on the table. There was only one line on the paper: "Dad's gone to find the sun for you." He would burn every memory of this farewell before the girl woke. Not because it was painful. But because only by forgetting her could he walk far enough. David turned around and walked into the hatchway of the Icarus. Behind him, the dark-red sun was rising on the horizon. Like an eye that refused to close.

Chapter One

The Ruins of Double Vision

01
David Keller's world began with a crack.
He was curled on the mouldering sofa, listening to the red sandstorm whip the concrete outside—a sound so fine it might have been sandpaper grinding against bone. Two-hundred-kilometre winds had scraped the entire surface into a sheet of dark red, and he sat at the deepest point of the half-subterranean bunker, listening to grains of sand sift through the gaps in the ventilation pipe, fall on the sheet metal, and produce an extremely faint, unbroken, brittle ticking.
Like someone counting coins.
He took off his glasses. The instant the light flooded in, pain exploded from the exact centre of his prefrontal lobe—a rusted crowbar shoved straight into the skull.
"Sss—"
He bit his lip shut. He didn't make a second sound. Emily was sleeping next door; her oxygen canister sighed once every four minutes—the compressor adjusting the pressure. He could hear that rhythm better than his own heartbeat.
In an ordinary person's retinas, the world is solid. But in David's eyes, the world was two photographs that could not be aligned. His left eye fixed on the digital clock in the corner, its numbers pulsing steadily—23:47, 23:47, 23:47—while his right drifted diagonally upward and landed on a patch of peeling paint on the ceiling.
Between the two images lay a grey-white zone that was nothing at all.
That was what the doctors called "visual noise." When the eye muscles could not align two images, the brain—to save processing power—simply discarded the information that fell into that gap. An ophthalmology professor at his elite university had once pointed a laser at his fundus photograph and explained: you see, here, here—the nerve endings are completely necrotic. You will never be able to see anything in that region.
What the professor didn't know was that the very gap he had discarded was the only place David could still see anything.
He held his breath, let his right eye relax, let his left go unfocused—
And then he saw it.
Between zero and one, at the edge where physical constants collapsed, in that grey-white region where nothing should have existed, a cluster of transparent symbols was drifting slowly.
It had no volume, occupied no space—like a shadow that had been flattened to two dimensions. But David knew who it was.
"Anna."
He breathed the name into the void.
02
The nutrition-paste wrapper was empty.
David turned the foil pouch over and scraped the last smear into his mouth with his index finger. The flavour landed somewhere between sweet and salty—as if two things that had no business being together had been forced into one.
"Dad, the medicine's run out." Emily pushed the door open and stood in the shadow. She was thin as a plant with its roots cut off; the oxygen mask pressed into her face had left dark-red indentations in the skin.
In this world ruled by the Zero Bureau, people like them were called "negative assets." Earth's resources were sufficient to support only one-tenth of the population; the remaining nine-tenths waited in this underground slum called the Cradle—waiting for the ship tickets that would never come.
03
The door was pushed open at 4:19 AM. Two men in white full-isolation protective suits stood in the doorway. On their chests was stitched the Zero Bureau's emblem: a circle with a line struck through it.
"Your brain is a broken hard drive," the man said. "Your strabismus lets you perceive things that don't exist. You are an error. But the Zero Bureau needs this error."
"What's the price?" David asked.
"You will die. After you enter the singularity you will be completely dismantled. Your memories, your emotions... all of it will be stripped away. You will become a perfect, logically self-consistent person. But you will no longer be you."
04
David stood at Emily's bedside for two minutes and fifty-nine seconds. He didn't wake her. He left a note on the table: "Dad's gone to find the sun for you."
05
The Icarus stood on the launch shaft, a slender ship, black all over, looking like a needle stuck into the ground.
"From the moment we launch, you will begin to lose your memories," the officer Su Qing said. "This is not a side effect—it is the fuel. The ship's engine runs on emotional mass."
David turned around and walked into the hatchway. Behind him, the dark-red sun was rising on the horizon. Like an eye that refused to close.
